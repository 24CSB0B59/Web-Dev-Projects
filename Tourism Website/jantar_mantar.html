Jantar Mantar

            
             The Jantar Mantar in Jaipur, Rajasthan, is an astronomical observation site, built in the 
             early 18th century. It comprises a collection of nineteen architectural astronomical 
             instruments. The monument was built by Rajput King Sawai Jai Singh II, and is 
             a UNESCO World Heritage site. 



             The Jantar Mantar features the world's largest stone sundial and is a significant 
             example of historical Indian astronomy. The instruments allow the observation of 
             astronomical positions with the naked eye. The Jantar Mantar was designed to measure 
             time, track celestial bodies, and observe the orbits of planets. Its primary purpose 
             was to gather scientific information and solve complex calculations.

Architecture and Instruments

            
    The Jantar Mantar site in Jaipur features a unique blend of science, art, and religion. 
    The numerous astronomical instruments are made primarily of stone and brass, each 
    carefully constructed to serve specific functions.

    Some of the key instruments at Jantar Mantar include:
    
    - *Samrat Yantra*: The world's largest stone sundial, which is used to measure time precisely.
    - *Jaiprakash Yantra*: A complex instrument used to determine the positions of celestial bodies.
    - *Ram Yantra*: Instrument used to measure altitudes of stars.
    - *Chakra Yantra*: This measures the declination of the Sun, coordinates of celestial bodies.
    - *Dakshin Bhitti Yantra*: This instrument is used to measure meridian passing of time.

    The architecture and instruments of Jantar Mantar are a testimony to the profound interest 
    in astronomy and the scientific advancements of ancient India. The site continues to attract. 
    astronomers, historians, architects, and tourists from all over the world.
